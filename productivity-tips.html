Foundations: the small habits that compound

Most "productivity hacks" fail because they ask you to overhaul your day at once. Compounding wins come from a small set of habits applied consistently: a daily intention, named time blocks, batched communication, single-task mode, planning the next step (not the whole project), small finish lines, and a brief daily review. Each habit removes a specific source of drag, and together they keep your attention pointed where it matters.

Strategies: how to plan and prioritize

Habits get you through the day. Strategies determine which days matter. Energy-based planning matches the hardest work to your peak hours. A simple priority matrix forces you to choose what is urgent and important versus what merely feels urgent. Realistic capacity planning prevents the "ambitious Monday, exhausted Wednesday" cycle. The strategies article covers each of these with concrete examples you can copy into any tool — including Today's Tasks.

Why a digital to-do list beats paper

Paper lists have a quiet, calming ritual. But once your work involves rearranging tasks, setting reminders, syncing across devices, searching past entries, or sharing context with others, paper costs you minutes every day in friction. A lightweight digital list keeps the calm parts of paper (just write the next thing down) and adds the parts that compound: instant rearrangement, durable search, and zero install or sync overhead.

The weekly review

A 20-minute weekly review is the single highest-leverage habit you can add to a to-do list practice. It clears stale items, re-ranks what's important for the next seven days, and gives you a calm Monday morning instead of a scrambled one. The weekly review checklist breaks the process into small steps with specific prompts so you can run it on a Friday afternoon without thinking.

FAQ

How many of these habits should I adopt at once?
One or two. The tips article covers seven habits, but adopting all seven at once is the same overload pattern that makes most productivity systems fail. Pick the one that addresses your biggest friction point this week, run it for two weeks, then add the next.
Do I need a calendar in addition to a to-do list?
Yes. The to-do list answers what; the calendar answers when. The strategies article covers how to pair them: time-block on the calendar for outcomes that need protected hours, keep everything else on the list.
How is Today's Tasks different from other free to-do apps?
Most free apps require an account and sync your data to a server. Today's Tasks runs entirely in your browser, stores tasks locally, and resets the visible list at midnight so stale items don't accumulate. See the digital lists article for the design rationale.
How do I do a weekly review without it taking an hour?
Use the checklist. The full review fits in 20–30 minutes once a week if you stick to the prompts: archive completed items, reschedule open items, choose three priorities for next week, and write a one-sentence reflection on what worked.
Where should I start?
If you're new to digital task management, start with How to Use Today's Tasks, set up your three lanes, and run the system for one week. Then read the tips and pick one habit to add. The strategies and weekly review articles compound from there.
